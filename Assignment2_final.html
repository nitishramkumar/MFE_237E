Now, the stock returns needs to be retrieved in order to find a possible trend of returns around the dividend payment date. At the same time, the daily SP500 return is retrieved and subtracted from the stock returns to get excess returns.

Stock data can be found in file CRSPQ.DSF


#Stock Returns
date.StartPeriod <- as.Date(min(stock.dividendEvents$PAYDT)) - 20
date.EndPeriod <- as.Date(max(stock.dividendEvents$PAYDT)) + 20
#Perform Inner join with DSP500 and get vwretd
sql.stockReturns <- paste0("SELECT permno,date,ret,(ret-vwretd) AS excessRet FROM CRSPQ.DSF dsf INNER JOIN CRSPQ.DSP500 sp ON dsf.date = sp.caldt WHERE date between '",format(date.StartPeriod,"%d%b%Y"),"'d AND '",format(date.EndPeriod,"%d%b%Y"),"'d AND permno IN (",paste0(stock.dividendEvents$PERMNO,collapse = ","),")")
stock.Returns <- getData(sql.stockReturns)
stock.Returns$DATE <- as.Date(stock.Returns$DATE)

Now, the mean returns at various relative dates from dividend payment is calculated. For example, for relative date 0, mean of all returns on the actual dividend payment date is calculated. Similar calculation is done for relative dates from -20 to 20.


stock.meanEventBasedReturns <- matrix(nrow = 41,ncol=2)
#For all relative dates,calculate mean of all stock returns for that date
for(count in -20:20){
  selectedStocksData <- stock.Returns[stock.Returns$DATE %in% (stock.dividendEvents$PAYDT + count),]
  selectedStocksDataRet <- selectedStocksData[!is.na(selectedStocksData$"excessRet"),]
  stock.meanEventBasedReturns[count+21,] <- c(count,mean(selectedStocksDataRet$"excessRet"))
}

Though no clear trend can be noticed(as there are various factors involved which are not covered in this activity), we can roughly notice the following

  1. The returns tend to reduce while approaching the dividend payment date. This will be due to the fact that after the ex-dividend date(generally 2 days before recording date, which in turn might be few days or a week before payment date), no new owner will receive the next dividend. This reduces the incentive to pay the premium price to buy the stock.

  2. The return roughly increases until around the ex-dividend date. This can be explained by the fact that investors, who are interested in receiving the dividends purchase the stocks by paying a premium.

  3. After an small dip in returns after the dividend payment (to adjust in price for the amount distributed), the returns increase. This might be because the market found the dividend distribution as a sign of company strength
